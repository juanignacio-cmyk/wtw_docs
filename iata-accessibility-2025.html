IATA Air Travel Accessibility Report — December 2025

Source: IATA Position Paper, December 2025

PDF: Saved locally

Key Stats

  • 1.3 billion people live with some form of disability (16% of world population, WHO)
  • 30% yearly increase in wheelchair assistance requests at airports
  • Requests more prevalent: India, US, UAE, Europe
  • Many requests from people without physical disabilities who lack confidence navigating busy airports, or need directional help (including age-related)
  • Airlines average profit per passenger: ~$7 (3-4 seats total profit per flight)

6 Key Issues

1. Wheelchair Assistance Surge (+30%/yr)

  • Significant and continues to grow yearly, +30% at bigger airports
  • Not sustainable for airlines and airports at current pace
  • Many requests from non-disabled passengers (confidence, age, navigation)
  • Need: better training for travel agents + ground staff on asking right questions
  • IATA calls on regulators to motivate airports to explore sustainable solutions
  • Encouraging passengers to inform airlines during BOOKING would help

2. Invisible Disabilities

  • Autism, dementia, etc. — not immediately apparent
  • Passengers hesitate to communicate conditions (fear of being denied boarding)
  • Good practices: sensory rooms, pre-travel info about what to expect
  • Need: advance notification system, on-board support, assistance during disruptions

3. Mobility Aid Damage (Wheelchairs)

  • Damage is devastating — devices are extensions of the body
  • Root cause: few mobility aids designed with air transport in mind
  • Issues: size/dimensions, weight (injury risk to staff), lithium-ion batteries (dangerous goods), airport design not optimized, cargo compartment incompatibility, information gaps
  • IATA 2023 guidance: electronic mobility aid tags, SSR codes, specialized ramp personnel, communication toolkit

4. Fragmented Regulations

  • Regulations are national/fragmented, not globally coordinated
  • Aviation is inherently international — needs multilateral collaboration
  • Current rules focus on eliminating complaints, not building universal access
  • IATA advocates for global standards (IATA + ICAO + ACI Accessibility Symposium, Montreal Dec 2024)

5. SSR Codes & Assistance Services

  • Passengers may not know what accommodations exist
  • 2024 IATA guidance for consistent assistance
  • SSR codes: standard protocol for communication between travel agencies, airlines, airports
  • When misused/inconsistent → entire travel experience disrupted

6. Website Accessibility

  • Accessibility info should be 1 click from airlines' landing page
  • IATA guidance: user-friendly gateway, clear criteria for assistance, early request emphasis
  • Streamlined approach for travelers with disabilities

Other Issues

  • Service Dogs: IATA wants global definition of "trained service dog" (exclude ESAs)
  • One Person One Fare: Airlines can't support free extra seats — route finances precarious (~$7 profit/passenger)
  • UN Tourism Action Agenda 2030: disability inclusion in tourism, ISO standards for tour operators

WTW Relevance

  • 30% growth validates the SURGING demand for accessibility in travel
  • The information gap = WTW's core value prop (structured accessibility data)
  • "Passengers who don't feel confident navigating" = the trust problem WTW solves for destinations
  • Fragmented regulations = opportunity for standardized verification (what AV does)
  • Airline websites improving but destinations lag behind → AV fills that gap
  • The wheelchair assistance surge is a SYMPTOM of the aging population trend (Google Travel 2050 connection: 1.57B people 65+ by 2050)
